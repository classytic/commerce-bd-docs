# Getting Started

The CRM module tracks the **path from a stranger to a paying customer** — inbound leads, qualified prospects, sales opportunities, and the activity history that ties it all together.

> **Heads up — dashboard UI is in development.** The CRM API + SDK are production-ready and integrated with the rest of the platform. Custom dashboards can already be built on top. The bundled BigBoss dashboard pages for CRM are coming in a future release.

---

## The Six Concepts

| Concept | What it is |
|---------|------------|
| **Lead** | An inbound prospect — name, email, source. Hasn't bought anything yet. |
| **Account** | A company or organization — industry, size, domain. |
| **Contact** | A person at an account — job title, email, phone. |
| **Opportunity** | A sales deal in progress — amount, stage, expected close date. |
| **Activity** | Work done on a lead/opportunity — call, email, meeting, task. |
| **Note** | Free-text comment on any record — not a task, just context. |

The flow:

```
Inbound form/email   →  Lead created
Outreach + qualify   →  Lead activities, status: contacted → qualified
Found a real deal    →  Convert lead → Account + Contact + Opportunity
Move through pipeline →  Opportunity stages: Qualify → Propose → Negotiate
Deal closes          →  Win (Customer becomes "active") or Lose (with loss reason)
```

---

## Lead vs. Customer — Where the Line Is

The CRM lives **upstream** of the existing Customers module:

- A **Lead** is someone you're hoping to sell to. They might be a fit, might not.
- A **Contact** is a real person at a company you're working with — created when a lead converts.
- A **Customer** (in the Customers module) is created when an opportunity is **won**. The CRM's `crm:opportunity.won` event automatically updates `Customer.crm.stage = 'active'`.

So Customers is the **post-sale** view; CRM is the **pre-sale** funnel that feeds it.

## A Typical Sales Week

Here's how a sales rep uses the CRM end-to-end:

1. **Triage new leads** — yesterday's inbound forms appear as `new` leads. The rep marks the ones worth pursuing as `contacted` after their first outreach.
2. **Qualify the good ones** — calls and emails happen; each becomes an **activity** logged against the lead. Promising ones move to `qualified`.
3. **Convert** — when a qualified lead has a real budget + decision-maker, the rep clicks **Convert**. The system creates an Account, a Contact, and an Opportunity in one atomic action.
4. **Move the deal** — the rep advances the opportunity through pipeline stages (`Qualify` → `Propose` → `Negotiate` → `Close`). Each stage has a default win probability.
5. **Close** — the rep marks the opportunity **Won** (deal signed!) or **Lost** (must include a loss reason). The Customer record auto-updates.
6. **Reflect** — at the end of the week, run reports on conversion rate by stage, win rate by loss reason, and per-rep activity volume.

## Why Loss Reasons Matter

When you mark an opportunity **Lost**, the system **requires** a loss reason — picked from a configurable list (Budget, Competitor, Timing, No-decision, etc.). This is intentional friction:

- Without it, "lost" data is useless — you can't tell a price problem from a timing problem
- With it, a quarter of "lost" data tells you exactly where your funnel leaks
- Reports group lost opportunities by reason so you can fix the actual problem

## Branch Scoping

CRM data is **per-branch** via `x-organization-id`:

- Each branch has its own leads, accounts, opportunities, pipelines, loss reasons
- Switching branches in the dashboard switcher shows that branch's CRM
- A single nationwide deal is owned by ONE branch — the one that closes it

If you have a head office handling enterprise deals while branches handle regional retail, run the head office's CRM in the head-office branch. Other branches focus on their local funnel.
